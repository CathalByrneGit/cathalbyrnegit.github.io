7. The Capture Cycle: Government Failure and the Lobby Dividend

The Irish housing crisis is now in its second decade. Five housing ministers, four major housing plans, and over €46 billion in committed public expenditure haven’t resolved it. This persistent failure is the predictable outcome of a regulatory capture dynamic in which the industry that benefits most from the status quo has acquired disproportionate influence over the policy responses to it.

7.2 The Lobby Density: Who Is Shaping the Response

The State’s Register of Lobbying, a public record of representations made to designated public officials — provides a direct measure of who has access to housing policy formation. The pattern is unambiguous.

**Construction Industry Federation (CIF): ** The single most active lobbyist on housing matters in Ireland. Filed 61 representations to Government in a single year on housing issues alone, and the highest number of returns of any organisation over the two-year period 2021–2023. Regularly meets local authority officials — nine separate meetings with Cork City and County Councils over two years. The CIF simultaneously chairs the body to which the Government proposed delegating the builder registration system, a dual role (industry advocate and regulatory body) that would be structurally prohibited in most comparable democracies.

**Property Industry Ireland (PII / Ibec): ** The employers’ body division representing institutional developers and investment funds. Filed 88 lobbying returns in two years. Sent a policy paper directly to the Minister of State for Housing proposing ‘wide-ranging changes’ to judicial review rules that would reduce the ability to challenge planning decisions in court, a reform that directly benefits developers facing planning objections.

**Irish Institutional Property (IIP): ** Represents large developers and investment funds including Cairn Homes and Ires REIT, Ireland’s largest private landlord. Met Department of Finance officials to warn that judicial reviews were creating ‘additional cost and risk’ and to lobby for fast-track planning reform. Minutes of these meetings (released under Freedom of Information) show department officials advising the lobby group on how proposed reforms would reduce legal challenges against housing plans.

When the Government convened a housing conference in January 2023 in response to record homelessness figures, the guest list was dominated by the same bodies: CIF (four representatives), IIP (three representatives), and PII — each given a seat at the table to propose ‘five ideas for how delivery of Housing for All can be accelerated.’ Civil society housing organisations, tenant advocacy groups, and public procurement specialists were marginal presences at best.

The Irish Examiner article of 5 June 2026 (which prompted Section 7.6 of this report) itself provides a further data point. Patsy Supple, chair of the CIF’s Cork branch, is quoted citing a KPMG report commissioned by Croí Cónaithe: ‘1% of developers are delivering 30% of the units. The small to medium-sized enterprise, less than 50 units per annum, represented 90% in numbers terms of developers and they were only delivering 29% of the housing.’ This finding — that the market is dominated by a small number of large developers — directly contradicts the lobby narrative that the sector is composed of struggling small firms, and it comes from data the CIF’s own Cork chair presented at an industry conference. The report it is drawn from was commissioned by the same state scheme (Croí Cónaithe) that pays €135,000 per unit to those large developers.

7.3 The Policy Fingerprints of Lobby Capture

Help-to-Buy: A Decade of Captured Subsidy

Help-to-Buy was conceived in 2016 as a temporary scheme costing €130 million over three years. It has been extended repeatedly and will remain in place until at least 2029. Cumulative claims approved have reached €1.3 billion. The Parliamentary Budget Office found that one-third of recipients didn’t need the scheme to meet their deposit requirement — pure deadweight cost to the exchequer. The Mazars review found almost half of funds spent fell into this deadweight category. The Central Bank and the ESRI both warned that the scheme inflates house prices by increasing buyer purchasing power without increasing supply. None of these findings resulted in reform. The CIF and allied bodies lobbied for each extension; each extension was granted.

The United Kingdom provides a direct precedent for where this trajectory leads. Persimmon, a British developer heavily dependent on the UK Help to Buy scheme, reported profits of £1.1 billion in 2018 (the highest in the housebuilder’s history) while its chief executive was awarded a bonus initially valued at £131 million. Critics noted that fewer homes were being built than before the financial crisis despite the taxpayer subsidy. The scheme had not increased supply; it had increased developer margins. Ireland’s Help-to-Buy operates through identical mechanics.

The UK precedent has since acquired a second chapter. In February 2024, the UK Competition and Markets Authority opened an investigation into seven major housebuilders — Barratt Redrow, Bellway, Berkeley Group, Bloor Homes, Persimmon, Taylor Wimpey and Vistry — over suspected exchanges of competitively sensitive information, including the prices at which homes were sold, the number of property viewings, and the incentives offered to buyers. In October 2025 the CMA formally accepted binding commitments from the seven firms, including a combined £100 million payment to affordable housing programmes, the largest payment ever secured by the CMA through a commitments package — without any admission of wrongdoing and without the investigation reaching a finding on whether competition law was broken. Members of the House of Lords criticised the settlement for closing the inquiry before it could produce ‘evidence-led answers about whether the business models’ of the major housebuilders were themselves the problem. Respondents to the CMA’s consultation asked for the information-sharing ban to be extended to land acquisition and land banking, citing collusion concerns; the CMA declined, noting these were outside the investigation’s scope. The same firm that extracted record profits from Help-to-Buy thus appears, seven years later, among a group of dominant builders resolving a price-information probe with a payment rather than a verdict, a documented illustration of how concentration, once achieved, is managed rather than corrected.

Sources: CMA, ‘Affordable housing set to benefit from £100 million following CMA probe’, July 2025; CMA final acceptance of commitments, 30 October 2025 (Construction News, 31 October 2025; Housing Today, 3 November 2025). House of Lords exchange: Hansard, 15 July 2025. Persimmon 2018 results and CEO bonus: widely reported, including UK national press, 2018–2019.

Strategic Housing Developments: Fast-Track as Favour

The Strategic Housing Development (SHD) system, introduced in 2017, created a fast-track planning process for large residential schemes that bypassed local authority planning boards and went directly to An Bord Pleanála. Property Industry Ireland has publicly claimed credit for selling the concept to government. The system was subsequently found to have produced significant legal uncertainty through a wave of judicial reviews, the very outcome that the industry had sought to prevent by removing local authority oversight in the first place. It was ultimately abolished. The successor system, the Large-Scale Residential Development (LSRD) mechanism, replicates several of its features.

Residential Zoned Land Tax: Lobbied Into Deferral

The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT), a levy on serviced, zoned development land to penalise hoarding — was legislated in 2021 as part of Housing for All. Before implementation, the Irish Home Builders Association (a CIF division), Property Industry Ireland, the Irish Farmers’ Association, and the Big Four accounting firms (Grant Thornton, PwC, KPMG, Deloitte) all lobbied for exemptions and deferrals. Freedom of Information records obtained by the Irish Times confirm the coordinated nature of these representations. The tax was deferred, its scope narrowed, and as of 2024 had generated a fraction of its projected revenue.

The RZLT’s fate has precedent: it’s the third consecutive Irish land-activation tax to die in implementation. The Derelict Sites Levy, on the statute book since the Derelict Sites Act 1990, is notoriously under-enforced: local authority returns and repeated media analysis have documented that only a small fraction of levies due are ever collected, with some authorities collecting nothing at all in given years despite registered derelict sites on their books. The Vacant Site Levy, introduced in 2015 at 3% and raised to 7%, was so weakly enforced (narrow registers, successful appeals, minimal collection) that it was abolished and replaced by the RZLT, which was then itself deferred and diluted before its first full collection. Three statutes, three decades, one pattern: Ireland legislates land taxes and doesn’t collect them. The pattern matters because it converts a common counter-argument into supporting evidence. The objection ‘we already have land taxes’ is true, and the documented systematic non-enforcement of all of them is exactly why Intervention 1’s design pairs the levy with automatic, self-executing mechanisms rather than discretionary local authority enforcement. A land tax that depends on the enforcement enthusiasm of the bodies that haven’t enforced its three predecessors is a press release, not a policy.

Sources: Derelict Sites Act 1990; local authority derelict sites returns and collection rates as reported in national media analyses, 2019–2024. Urban Regeneration and Housing Act 2015 (Vacant Site Levy); abolition and replacement via Finance Act 2021 (RZLT). RZLT deferrals: Finance Acts 2022–2024; Irish Times FOI reporting on lobbying, 2022–2024.

The 9% VAT Rate: A Fourth Subsidy Layer

Budget 2026 introduced a reduction in VAT on apartment sales from 13.5% to 9%. The measure was subsequently extended — via a November 2025 financial resolution, after further industry representations to the Department of Finance, to cover forward-funding arrangements, whereby a site is sold and construction services are contracted separately. Forward funding is the dominant financing structure for large institutional apartment developments, including the Marina Depot and Blackrock Villas projects documented in Section 8. The BDO analysis confirms explicitly that the extension was made because the initial scope ‘represented a significant limitation’ and that ‘further consideration by the Department of Finance’ resolved it.

The financial mechanics of the VAT reduction, applied to the documented Cork developments, are instructive. On the LDA’s forward purchase of 337 Marina Depot apartments at €445,133 per unit, the reduction from 13.5% to 9% represents a saving of approximately €20,000 per apartment, or approximately €6.7 million on that transaction alone. At Blackrock Villas (274 units, average price approximately €310,000), the per-unit saving is roughly €14,000, or approximately €3.8 million in total. These savings flow to Glenveagh as the developer and seller — stacked on top of the Croí Cónaithe subsidy (€135,000/unit), Help-to-Buy (up to €30,000/unit to buyers), and the First Home Scheme (up to 30% of purchase price). The VAT reduction is a fifth layer of state support on transactions already receiving four others, benefiting the same concentrated group of large developers confirmed by KPMG’s own research to deliver 30% of all units.

The measure’s framing as social policy deserves scrutiny. The EU VAT Directive amendment of 2022 (Council Directive 2022/542) permits member states to apply reduced VAT rates to housing ‘as part of a social policy, as defined by the Member States.’ Ireland is applying the reduced rate to market-rate apartment sales by listed companies returning hundreds of millions to shareholders — Glenveagh alone returned over €445 million since 2021. The social policy intent of the EU directive and Ireland’s application of it aren’t obviously the same thing. Neither Grant Thornton, BDO, EY, nor KPMG (all of whom published analysis of the measure) address this tension in their commentary.

Sources: Budget 2026 — Department of Finance, October 2025. Finance Act 2025 — extension to forward funding, November 2025. BDO Ireland, ‘9% VAT Rate for Apartments: Helping to Address Ireland’s Housing Crisis Through Tax Policy’, February 2026 (bdo.ie). Grant Thornton Ireland, ‘9% VAT rate: A bold step to support apartment supply’ (grantthornton.ie). EY Ireland, ‘Finance Bill 2025: Extension of 9% VAT Rate for Apartments’, December 2025 (ey.com). KPMG Ireland, ‘Property & Construction — Budget 2026’ (kpmg.com). Council Directive (EU) 2022/542, Official Journal of the European Union. The State on All Four Sides of the Transaction

One further structural feature completes the capture picture, and it has no parallel in any other Irish industry. Through Home Building Finance Ireland (HBFI), a state lender established in 2019 and capitalised through the Ireland Strategic Investment Fund to provide development finance to housebuilders, the Irish state now occupies every side of the residential development transaction. The state lends developers the capital to build (HBFI). The state subsidises the construction itself where the developer declares a viability gap (Croí Cónaithe). The state inflates the purchaser’s capacity to pay the asking price (Help-to-Buy, the First Home Scheme). And the state purchases the completed output directly at full post-uplift market prices (LDA forward funding). Four distinct state interventions, on four sides of the same transaction — financing the supply, subsidising the supply, subsidising the demand, and constituting the demand.

At the centre of this square sits the one value flow the state doesn’t touch: the planning-permission uplift, created by the state’s own zoning and permission decisions, captured privately at margins of 76–90% of value added, and returned to the state only in the form of the purchase prices and viability gaps it then pays again to bridge. No other sector enjoys this arrangement. The state doesn’t lend to, subsidise, demand-support, and bulk-purchase from the aircraft leasing industry, the pharmaceutical industry, or the agri-food industry simultaneously. The configuration exists in housing because each side of the square was constructed separately, each in response to a symptom, each lobbied for or welcomed by the sector, and because no institution within the state apparatus, as Section 7.7 documents, was tasked with viewing the four sides as a single structure. Seen whole, the structure has a precise description: the state has socialised every risk and cost of the development model while leaving its central revenue (the planning rent) fully privatised.

Sources: HBFI — Home Building Finance Ireland Act 2018; HBFI annual reports (lending to homebuilders, capitalised via ISIF). Croí Cónaithe, Help-to-Buy, First Home Scheme, LDA forward funding: as sourced in Sections 7.3 and 8.

7.4 The Failure Premium: How Inaction Produces Dependence

The deepest structural problem is a feedback loop in which government failure produces the conditions for greater industry leverage, which produces further failure. The mechanism runs as follows.

**Step 1 — Policy failure: ** Government sets a housing delivery target. Industry lobbies successfully for the conditions under which it will deliver (subsidies, fast-track planning, reduced standards, demand-side supports). Targets are missed anyway, because the structural land-value problem isn’t addressed.

**Step 2 — Crisis deepens: ** Homelessness rises, rents rise, waiting lists lengthen, and political pressure intensifies. The government needs to be seen acting urgently.

**Step 3 — Increased dependence: ** Having dismantled or underfunded direct public delivery capacity (local authority construction teams, direct procurement infrastructure), the state has no mechanism to deliver housing except through the private development sector. It is structurally dependent on the same industry whose lobbying shaped the conditions of failure.

**Step 4 — Capture deepens: ** Industry negotiating power increases. Each new housing plan is developed in closer consultation with industry bodies. The policy menu narrows toward options industry can support (more subsidies, more planning facilitation, reduced levies) and away from structural interventions industry opposes: land value capture, consolidated accounts requirements, public direct procurement.

**Step 5 — Repeat: ** The new plan fails to close the structural gap for the same reasons as the previous one. The cycle repeats with escalating public expenditure and escalating developer surplus.

This dynamic is exactly what the financial data in this report measures. The €8.167 billion in NACE 411 developer profit extracted between 2015 and 2024 isn’t a side-effect of housing policy; it is, in significant part, its product. Each demand-side subsidy, each fast-track planning facilitation, each deferred land tax, each missed supply target that keeps demand pressure elevated, has been a direct transfer from the public housing budget into the developer surplus column.

7.5 The Escalating Cost of Doing Nothing Structural

The financial cost of capture compounds over time. Rebuilding Ireland cost €10 billion over six years. Housing for All is projected to cost €36 billion over nine years, a 3.6× increase in expenditure for a programme that replicates the structural approach of its predecessor. The ESRI’s structural demand estimate of 44,000 units per year against an actual 2024 delivery of 30,147 implies a deficit accumulation of roughly 14,000 units per year. At current market prices, each unbuilt unit represents both a household in continued housing insecurity and a future liability for the state in emergency accommodation, HAP payments, and health and social care costs.

The Housing Commission’s finding of a 256,000-unit underlying deficit (published in May 2024) has no dedicated clearance mechanism in any current government programme. At the pace of delivery above-target achievable within the current framework (approximately 3,000–6,000 units per year surplus over minimum need), the deficit would take 40–80 years to clear. No government plan has engaged honestly with this arithmetic.

The section that follows sets out the structural interventions required to break this cycle. They aren’t radical in the sense of being economically untested — each has a working EU precedent. They are radical only in the sense of being structurally threatening to the extraction system whose lobby has successfully prevented their implementation for a decade.

7.6 The Epistemological Problem: When the Lobby Becomes the Expert

Beyond the documented lobbying activity lies a more subtle and arguably more consequential form of capture: the systematic adoption of industry framing as neutral analytical language in media, government, and professional commentary on the housing crisis. The CIF is Ireland’s most active housing lobbyist and simultaneously one of the most frequently quoted sources in Irish housing journalism, in the majority of cases without characterisation of its financial interest in the positions it advocates. But the CIF isn’t the only institution through which this framing operates. The five-firm case study of the 9% VAT rate illustrates a parallel and in some respects more influential channel: the professional services ecosystem.

The CIF’s own communications strategy makes this explicit. Its media agency case study (published by MediaHQ) states that the CIF ‘reacts to the ever-evolving news cycle around housing’ and ‘proactively releases 6 or 7 pre-planned reports every year,’ having sent 652 press releases since 2016. It names the Irish Times, RTÉ, the Examiner, Business Post, and the Irish Independent as regular outlets.

The Viability Gap: A Lobby Term Adopted as Neutral Fact

The ‘viability gap’ originated in developer and CIF communications. It has since been adopted verbatim by government press releases, Oireachtas debates, Central Bank research, EU State Aid approval documents, and mainstream media coverage, in almost all cases without the observation that the gap is partly an artefact of the land-value extraction system documented in this report. The EU Commission’s own State Aid approval of Croí Cónaithe Cities (February 2023) uses the phrase ‘the so-called viability gap’, the parenthetical ‘so-called’ acknowledging it is adopting industry terminology. Irish media coverage dropped the hedge entirely.

What the Glenveagh Cork case study in Section 8 demonstrates is that the viability gap at Blackrock Villas (requiring €135,000 per unit in state subsidy) exists on a development by a company that returned over €445 million to shareholders since 2021 and sold 337 apartments to the LDA at €445,133 per unit on a site acquired for €15 million. The viability gap and the margin extraction are two faces of the same system. Reporting one without the other isn’t neutral; it is structurally partial.

The Coveney Problem: Industry Consultants as Independent Voices

The Irish Examiner article of 5 June 2026 quotes Simon Coveney saying the Irish apartment market is ‘effectively broken.’ The observation is correct. What the article doesn’t note is that Coveney was speaking at the CIF Southern Construct Summit 2026, an industry conference organised by the Construction Industry Federation. In the CIF’s own conference materials, he is identified as ‘Managing Director, Waterfront Advisory.’ In media coverage, he is identified primarily as ‘former Government minister.’ None of this invalidates the observation. But it matters who is characterising the break and in whose interest.

The Pattern in the Data

The CIF dynamic is structural, not exceptional. The CIF chair Michael Kelleher — simultaneously group operations director of the O’Flynn Group, a major developer — told a CIF summit at Croke Park in April 2025 that there is a ‘chronic viability gap’ with 40,000 apartment permissions unactivated in Dublin. This was reported as a factual housing sector finding. The Irish Times tags page for ‘Construction Industry Federation’ returns dozens of results in which the CIF is the primary framing source for housing supply analysis, levy policy, planning reform, and affordability — the full range of contested policy questions on which the CIF has a direct financial interest. The Register of Lobbying confirms the CIF filed the most housing-related lobbying returns of any organisation over 2020–2022. The MediaHQ case study confirms 652 press releases since 2016. These facts together describe not a conspiracy but a system: a well-resourced lobby with a professional communications operation, generating analysis on questions where it has a direct financial stake, achieving consistent coverage in outlets that lack dedicated housing economics reporters with the time or resources to interrogate the underlying data.

This report uses CSO National Accounts, Eurostat SBS primary data, Land Registry records, and company annual reports to reach its conclusions. The sector the CIF represents extracted €8.167 billion in gross operating surplus between 2015 and 2024, at an average value margin of 83.1% — figures drawn from datasets the CIF’s member companies themselves report to the state. When the CIF characterises that sector as financially fragile and requiring sustained state support, and when that characterisation enters the media record as neutral expert analysis rather than advocacy, the public record is structurally incomplete. Naming that incompleteness is not a political act. It is an evidentiary one.

The Professional Services Channel: A Second Track

Parallel to the CIF’s direct lobbying and media strategy operates a second and arguably more influential framing channel: the Big Four accounting and professional services firms (Deloitte, EY, Grant Thornton, and KPMG) and other mid-tier practices such as BDO and Gilroy Gannon. These firms occupy a structurally different position from the CIF. They aren’t industry lobby groups and don’t present themselves as such. They are professional advisers who serve both private developer clients and, in many cases, state agencies including the Housing Finance Agency, the LDA, and the Department of Finance. Their housing analysis is published under the authority of professional expertise rather than industry advocacy.

Yet the same firms that coordinated with the CIF to lobby for RZLT exemptions and deferrals (confirmed by FOI-released records, Irish Times, 2022) simultaneously publish analysis that frames housing policy measures exclusively through the lens of developer viability. The five-firm response to the 9% VAT rate is a documented example. Grant Thornton calls the reduction ‘a bold step.’ BDO calls it ‘a welcome response.’ EY describes the process through which industry concerns were addressed in the Finance Bill, the extension from completed sales to forward-funding arrangements — as a clarification that ensures the measure ‘aligns with the practical way in which these developments are arranged.’ None of the five pieces asks to whom the €20,000 per-unit VAT saving flows, how it interacts with the €135,000 per-unit Croí Cónaíthe subsidy already in place, or whether the combined subsidy stack on a forward-funded apartment is consistent with the EU directive’s stated social policy intent.

KPMG’s position is particularly notable given its dual roles. KPMG authored the research commissioned by Croí Cónaíthe that confirmed 1% of developers deliver 30% of output — data that directly undermines the small-developer viability narrative. KPMG’s Budget 2026 property analysis simultaneously covers the VAT reduction as a straightforward measure to improve housing supply. KPMG also, per FOI records confirmed by the Irish Times, lobbied on behalf of developer clients for RZLT exemptions in the same period. The firm is simultaneously researcher, lobbyist, adviser to state schemes, and analyst of the measures it helps shape. This isn’t improper; it is the nature of professional services work in a small market with concentrated expertise. But it means that the professional services channel, like the CIF channel, reliably produces analysis that starts from the developer viability premise and works outward, rather than starting from the distributional data and working inward toward the question of who benefits.

This report uses CSO National Accounts, Eurostat SBS primary data, Land Registry records, and company annual reports. The sector the CIF represents, and whose interests the Big Four routinely serve, extracted €8.167 billion at an average 83.1% value margin — figures from datasets those companies’ own clients report to the state. When the CIF characterises that sector as financially fragile, when Grant Thornton calls a VAT subsidy ‘bold,’ when EY describes the extension of that subsidy to forward-funding arrangements as a clarification, and when none of these analyses are accompanied by the distributional question — who benefits, in what amount, in the context of what other supports, the public record is structurally incomplete. Naming that incompleteness is evidentiary work, not political work.
