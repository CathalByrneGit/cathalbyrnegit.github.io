8. Case Study: Glenveagh Properties in Cork — The System Made Concrete

The structural arguments in the preceding sections — land value capture, developer margin extraction, vertical integration, and regulatory capture — can appear abstract when expressed as sector-wide averages. This section makes them concrete through a single, fully documented case study: Glenveagh Properties plc and its two active Cork developments. Every figure is sourced to a primary document — Glenveagh’s own published results, official government press releases, LDA announcements, or Housing Agency records.

This case study does not allege improper conduct by Glenveagh. The company is operating within the law and within the policy framework the state has constructed. That’s the point.

8.1 Glenveagh: Who They Are

Glenveagh Properties plc (Euronext Dublin: GLV) is one of Ireland’s two listed homebuilders. Its Full Year Results 2025 (published 13 March 2026) record revenue of €926 million (a company record, up 7%), gross profit of €198 million at a 21.4% gross margin, profit before tax of €125 million, and capital returns to shareholders of over €445 million since 2021, including a €105 million share buyback completed in 2025 with a further programme launched in January 2026. The results statement records that the landbank was secured at an average cost of approximately €32,000 per unit — ‘less than 10% of net development value’ in the company’s own words — embedding spot margins of approximately 21%. The CEO attributes margin resilience explicitly to the company’s ‘market-leading landbank, planning expertise, and improved manufacturing capability.’ These are the company’s own descriptions of its position: the land was acquired at a small fraction of its development value, and the planning expertise to convert it is named alongside the landbank as the source of the margin.

Source: Glenveagh Properties plc Full Year Results 2025, 13 March 2026.

The state paid €445,133 per apartment, a price reflecting the full post-planning-permission market value. Had a hard land value capture levy been in place at rezoning, the planning uplift of ~€510 million would have been substantially returned to the public purse. Instead, the state paid for it twice: once by granting planning permission that created the value, and again by purchasing the output at a price that includes that value.

Two points of fairness should be stated explicitly, because the analysis doesn’t depend on suppressing them. First, Glenveagh didn’t acquire the site cheaply by any market standard: the €8.5m guide price was nearly doubled at a competitive NAMA sale process that attracted significant interest. There was no sweetheart deal, and this report does not suggest one. That is precisely what makes the case study an indictment of the framework rather than of the firm — the ~€510 million in value was created after a full market-price purchase, by the planning system alone, and captured privately because no mechanism existed to do otherwise. Second, securing the permission involved genuine effort, cost, and risk: the refusal of the larger O’Callaghan Properties scheme nearby demonstrates that permission isn’t granted automatically. But that observation supports the report’s framework rather than undermining it; it is the scarcity and discretionary nature of the permission that gives it its extraordinary value once granted. The harder the permission is to obtain, the larger the rent it confers on the holder.

The LDA’s Marina Depot price is also not an outlier within its own programme. The same Glenveagh announcement records the January 2025 sale of 139 apartments at Brownsbarn, Dublin, to the LDA for €52 million (approximately €374,000 per unit) as one of six active Partnerships projects. The forward-fund price band of roughly €375,000–€445,000 per apartment is the going rate at which the state purchases output whose underlying land cost, at Marina Depot, was €12,733 per unit.

8.3 Blackrock Villas: The Subsidy Circuit

Budget 2026’s 9% VAT rate (reduced from 13.5%) adds a fifth layer of state support to the Blackrock Villas transaction, stacking on top of the four mechanisms already documented. On a €292,500 one-bedroom apartment, the 4.5 percentage point VAT reduction represents approximately €13,163 in reduced tax on the developer’s sale. This saving flows directly to Glenveagh, not to the buyer. The forward-funding extension, confirmed via the November 2025 financial resolution after industry representations to the Department of Finance, ensures the rate applies to Glenveagh’s preferred transaction structure.

With the VAT saving included, up to approximately €265,000 of state support per unit can now be layered onto a €292,500 one-bedroom apartment, meaning the combined state contribution exceeds what a buyer borrowing at 90% LTV would receive as a mortgage on the same property. The Croí Cónaithe payment alone (€135,000) goes directly to Glenveagh regardless of whether the buyer accesses any demand-side scheme. The VAT saving (approximately €13,000 on this unit price) goes to Glenveagh as seller. The Help-to-Buy and First Home Scheme payments go to the buyer to clear Glenveagh’s asking price. Glenveagh’s own mortgage calculator, prominently featured on its website and in sponsored content, directs buyers through the process of combining all available schemes.

Two documentary details strengthen the record. The Blackrock Villas approval is confirmed by the government’s own press release: 274 owner-occupier apartments on the former Ursuline Convent grounds, the third approval nationally under Croí Cónaithe Cities and the second in Cork, with the Housing Agency listing first completions for mid-2026. And the per-unit subsidy figure used here is necessarily an estimate, because the Department of Housing doesn’t publish per-contract subsidy amounts, the €135,000 figure is the scheme-wide average derived from the disclosed maximum funding envelope (€78.7m) across the disclosed unit count (582). That a nine-figure viability subsidy programme doesn’t disclose what each developer receives per apartment is itself a finding: the analytical gap documented in Section 7.7 extends to the basic transparency of the subsidy architecture.

8.5 What This Case Study Proves — and What It Does Not

1. The viability gap is a planning-uplift artefact: The gap at Blackrock Villas exists in the context of ~€510 million in planning-permission value transferred to Glenveagh at Marina Depot at no cost. The same planning system that creates developer wealth at one site creates the conditions requiring state subsidy at another.

2. The state pays full post-uplift prices: The LDA paid €445,133 per apartment — reflecting the full planning-permission value, not the €12,733 pre-uplift land cost. The taxpayer funds the uplift twice.

3. Subsidies are stacked, not substituted: Croí Cónaithe to the developer, the 9% VAT saving to the developer on sale, Help-to-Buy to the buyer, First Home Scheme to bridge the affordability gap — all additive across five layers. The Croí Cónaithe payment and the VAT saving are made regardless of whether the buyer accesses any demand-side scheme. The total confirmed and estimated state exposure across both Cork Glenveagh developments now exceeds €213 million, updated to include the Budget 2026 VAT reduction.

4. The model is explicitly designed for repetition: Glenveagh’s Partnerships business segment (the division built around forward sales to state bodies) grew its revenue from €238 million in 2024 to €381 million in 2025, 60% growth in a single year, at an 18.2% gross margin, with Marina Depot its sixth active project (Glenveagh Full Year Trading Statement, January 2026). The company’s own results describe it as ‘an established partner of choice for the State.’ In this model the state isn’t the last resort; it’s the business model.

5. The marketing completes the circuit: Glenveagh’s own mortgage calculator and sponsored content in The Journal (May 2025) actively direct buyers toward Help-to-Buy and the First Home Scheme. The developer isn’t a passive beneficiary of state demand-side support; it systematically incorporates those subsidies into its sales mechanism, effectively ensuring that the state subsidy raises the effective price buyers can pay, which maintains the sticker price that requires the supply-side subsidy in the first place.

What the case study doesn’t prove is any wrongdoing. Glenveagh is operating within the law, winning competitive tenders, delivering homes that wouldn’t otherwise be built under the current framework, and generating returns for its shareholders. The problem the case study documents is a policy framework that has made the state the indispensable funding partner of a private company’s growth strategy, not corporate misconduct, without capturing any of the planning value that made the partnership necessary in the first place.

Simon Coveney, speaking at the CIF’s Southern Construct conference in May 2026, described the Irish apartment market as ‘effectively broken.’ He is correct. But the break isn’t a natural market failure; it is the predictable outcome of a planning system that privatises the value it creates, a subsidy architecture that bridges the gap that privatisation produces, and a procurement model that pays market prices for output on land the state enabled. The Glenveagh Cork case study shows the system working exactly as currently designed.
